As the digital vanguard, AI often signals utopian futures across industries, promising transformative changes and unparalleled efficiencies. Yet, when thrust into practical application within education, the results can sometimes underscore the vast stretch between promise and utility. IBM's Watson supercomputer provides a prescient tale in this arena, especially in its attempts to augment tutoring through AI.

IBM's Watson, renowned for its natural language processing prowess and most famously pitted against human champions on Jeopardy!, was enlisted to revolutionize tutoring. The Watson Tutoring System was designed with an ambition to surpass human capabilities by leveraging its broad data comprehension prowess. The overarching belief was that Watson could handle numerous problems and their derivatives simultaneously, surpassing any limitations humans might encounter due to fatigue or oversight.

The practical application of this tremendous computational resource was carried out in a comparative study with human tutors at the University of California, Berkeley. It might seem logical to assert that Watson, building upon its vast, accessible reservoir of up-to-date knowledge, would provide superior tutoring. Unfortunately, the results diverged from this expectation. The performance metrics post-deployment showed that students assisted by Watson demonstrated only negligible improvements compared to a control group engaged with human tutors. These outcomes challenge the notion that AI, as advanced as Watson, can seamlessly replace the nuanced roles human tutors play.

Critiquing IBM's methodology, some proponents suggest that tangible progress in AI tutoring should consider metrics beyond mere test scores. Learning is dimensional, influenced by emotional, psychological, and intellectual stimuli, where human tutors often intuit and react in real-time to a student's afflicting barriers—an area where Watson might be programmatically deficient. The assumption that Watson’s large-scale data processing would translate directly into higher test scores may overlook fundamental aspects of human learning which are, as of now, difficult to quantify and systematically implement within an AI framework.

Moreover, research from esteemed institutions like Carnegie Mellon University highlights the exceptional complexity of modeling effective AI tutors. True understanding requires not only domain-specific knowledge but also a deep grasp of context, learner's background, and current abilities. Developing an AI system that comprehensively incorporates these elements involves arduous advances in AI, particularly within realms like deep learning and natural language processing, which require extensive training and robust datasets.

In the larger discourse of educational technology, these findings serve as a reminder and a call for a balanced view. It’s crucial to harness AI’s potential mindfully, recognizing its current limitations. Effective AI tutoring systems need to evolve substantially before they can genuinely replicate or exceed the multifaceted roles of human tutors.

To envision and work towards a future where AI tutors sustain or augment educational outcomes, stakeholders must prioritize developing and nurturing the facets of AI that address both the mechanistic and humanistic aspects of learning. While grand visions of AI transforming education are enticing, a pragmatic yet optimistic approach may be more appropriate, acknowledging that human touch elements in education are not easily replicable by even the most sophisticated AI systems in the current technological landscape.

In conclusion, IBM’s Watson's journey into the world of tutoring encapsulates the nuanced hurdles of transferring theoretical AI capabilities into effective educational practice. It heralds a cautionary tale; a narrative urging technologists and educators to collaboratively iterate more responsive, insightful AI tools that genuinely complement educational methodologies, without underestimating the value of human interaction within learning paradigms.
