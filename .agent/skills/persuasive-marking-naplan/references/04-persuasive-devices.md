# Persuasive Devices Criterion (0-4 points)

**Skill focus:** The use of a range of persuasive devices to enhance the writer's position and persuade the reader.

**NOTES:** About a page of writing is needed to consider sustained use.

---

## Score: 0
**Category:** No evidence or insufficient evidence
- Symbols or drawings
- Writes in wrong genre
- Copies topic only

---

## Score: 1
**Category:** Statement of opinion or minimal devices
- Uses a statement or statements of personal opinion
**OR**
- Uses one or two instances of persuasive devices (may be same type)

**Characteristics:**
- Opinion may appear confused or contradictory
- Uses only simple devices (I think ... very, very)
- "I reckon ... should ... because" forms one instance of a persuasive device

---

## Score: 2
**Category:** Three or more instances, at least two types
- Uses three or more instances of persuasive devices that support the writer's position (at least two types)
- May have more devices than required but these are ineffective

---

## Score: 3
**Category:** Some effective devices, not sustained
- Uses some devices that persuade
- Use is effective but not sustained (may also include some ineffective use)

**Characteristics:**
- There are many devices that can be used to persuade a reader
- Effective devices are appropriate to the style of argument and may appeal to one or more of the reader's reason, values or emotions

---

## Score: 4
**Category:** Sustained and effective use
- Sustained and effective use of persuasive devices
- Devices are used strategically throughout the text
- Multiple types of devices employed effectively

---

## Common Persuasive Devices

### Appeals
- **Appeal to reason (logos)** - Logic, facts, statistics, evidence
- **Appeal to emotions (pathos)** - Fear, sympathy, pride, hope
- **Appeal to ethics (ethos)** - Authority, credibility, moral values

### Rhetorical Devices
- **Rhetorical questions** - Questions that don't require an answer
- **Repetition** - Repeating words/phrases for emphasis
- **Rule of three** - Three points, examples, or arguments
- **Emotive language** - Words chosen to evoke emotional response
- **Exaggeration/Hyperbole** - Overstating for effect

### Evidence and Support
- **Expert opinion** - Citing authorities or experts
- **Statistics and facts** - Numerical evidence
- **Examples and anecdotes** - Specific cases or stories
- **Comparisons and analogies** - Comparing to familiar situations

### Language Techniques
- **Modal verbs** - Should, must, could, would
- **Inclusive language** - We, us, our (including reader)
- **Direct address** - You (speaking to reader)
- **Counter-arguments** - Acknowledging opposing views then refuting them
- **Cause and effect** - Showing consequences
